# FX, Cross-Currency Basis, and Dollar Funding


---

## 1. Currency as the stock price of a nation’s claims

Spot FX is the relative price of two monies. Under the surface, the enormous market is about **funding**: who needs dollars (or euros, yen) to roll assets, hedge issuance, and clear trade.

If you only forecast FX from growth differentials, you will miss **balance-sheet and basis** shocks that move currencies and bond markets together.

---

## 2. Covered interest parity (CIP) — theory

In frictionless markets, the forward FX rate is pinned by interest rate differentials so that covered (hedged) borrowing in one currency vs another yields no arbitrage.

**CIP intuition:** borrow in currency A, swap to B, invest in B, cover with forward—should earn no free lunch after rates.

---

## 3. Cross-currency basis (XCCY) — reality

The **cross-currency basis** is the extra cost (often quoted as a spread on one floating leg) required to make the hedged exchange of funding fair in real markets.

Empirically, the basis is often **not zero**. A common educational reading when the dollar basis is “negative” in market parlance: non-U.S. entities **pay up** to obtain dollar funding on a hedged basis—global dollar scarcity relative to unconstrained arbitrage capital.

### Why CIP breaks
1. **Global structural demand for USD** (trade invoicing, dollar debt, commodity trade).  
2. **Dealer balance-sheet constraints** (Basel leverage, G-SIB, internal limits)—arbitrage is not infinite.  
3. **Corporate issuance patterns** (e.g. reverse yankee style flows: issue in EUR, swap to USD) that push bases.  
4. **Year-end window dressing** when banks shrink dollar lending.

When the basis widens violently, foreign banks funding USD assets via swaps face higher costs—or cannot roll—and may sell assets (including Treasuries). **FX funding stress becomes UST volatility.**

---

## 4. FX swaps market

FX swaps (near leg + far leg) dominate short-term cross-currency liquidity. Educational points:

- Enormous daily notional; much is short tenor (&lt; 1 week).  
- Used to roll hedges and manage liquidity.  
- **Rollover risk:** if the market freezes (2008, March 2020 lessons), foreign banks scramble for dollars; central bank swap lines become the backstop narrative.

---

## 5. Non-deliverable forwards (NDFs)

For currencies with capital controls or limited onshore access (examples historically include various EM names):

- NDFs settle in USD (or another deliverable) on a fixing.  
- **Fixing risk:** official fixings can be managed; basis between NDF and onshore can embed devaluation / convertibility stress.  
- Educational use: NDF premium vs onshore as a pressure gauge—not a casual retail product.

---

## 7. Key takeaways

1. FX markets clear funding as much as relative growth stories.  
2. CIP is a benchmark; XCCY basis measures frictions and dollar demand.  
3. Dealer constraints keep arbitrage imperfect.  
4. FX swap freezes transmit to bond markets.  
5. NDFs price convertibility and policy risk.
